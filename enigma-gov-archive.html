Government agencies generate some of the most detailed, authoritative records about how businesses operate in America. Business registrations, health inspections, environmental compliance filings, professional licenses, UCC liens, employee benefit plans, building permits, campaign finance disclosures — thousands of agencies, at every level of government, document how businesses form, operate, and interact with the public.

The problem is that no one — including the government itself — can use this information coherently. Federal agencies aren't connected to each other. State systems add another layer of complexity. Municipal records fragment even further. A restaurant's health inspection in New York looks nothing like its food safety record in Chicago, which looks nothing like its liquor license in Maryland — even though all three describe the same business.

The data that should provide the most complete picture of a business is scattered across roughly 7,000 datasets, each with its own schema, format, and update cadence.

At Enigma, we've spent years building structured data on U.S. businesses — entity resolution, verified identities, card revenue signals. Our structured graph draws from a few hundred high-value sources that we've mapped, standardized, and resolved into a unified view of business identity. This is the data that already powers our KYB and compliance products.

But thousands of government databases contain information that our structured approach has not yet reached. The variety of schemas across 7,000 sources makes traditional one-by-one integration impractical at that scale. A different approach was needed for this long tail of valuable but highly disparate data — a system that could draw from the power of these records without requiring each dataset to be individually mapped into our graph.

That's the Enigma Gov Archive.

Section 01

What We Built

Gov Archive is a growing collection of 1.5 billion government records across approximately 7,000 datasets from federal, state, and local agencies — business licenses, regulatory filings, legal records, permits, inspections, and more. The Archive now powers deeper capabilities across Enigma's products, surfacing signals from government filings that were previously out of reach.

Two things make it work:

Pillar 01

The Archive Itself

1.5 billion records and growing. Four months ago the archive held 300 million. The collection spans business licenses, OSHA citations, EPA compliance records, state cannabis licensing, UCC filings, building permits, government contract data, and thousands of other filing types.

Each source refreshes on its own cadence — some daily, some weekly, some monthly — and the system tracks freshness at the dataset level.

Pillar 02

Entity Resolution as Foundation

Every search is grounded in Enigma's verified business identity — the same infrastructure that powers our KYB and compliance products. The system doesn't just search text. It knows which business it's looking for, including every name variation, every legal entity, every DBA.

This is what separates Gov Archive from pointing a search engine at government websites. Without identity grounding, government records are mainly noise. With it, they become signal.

Now

We're resolving Gov Archive records directly into the entity graph, so a cannabis license found in a state database or an EIN discovered in an incentive filing becomes part of the verified business identity itself — not just a search result alongside it.

Section 02

How It Works

When you query a business through Enigma's products, the system starts with what Enigma already knows. It resolves the business to a verified entity in our graph and expands to every known name variation — brand names, legal entity names, DBAs. It then searches across 1.5 billion government records using all of those variations.

An LLM evaluates the results for relevancy, deduplicates across sources and jurisdictions, and synthesizes what it finds into a comprehensive profile — with every data point traced to the specific government agency, dataset, and filing it came from.

Query Sequence
1 Resolve Business resolved to verified entity in Enigma graph
2 Expand Every name variation — brand, legal entity, DBA
3 Search 1.5B records queried across all variations
4 Evaluate LLM deduplicates, scores relevancy across jurisdictions
5 Cite Every data point traced to agency, dataset, and filing

What used to require hours of manual research across dozens of government websites now takes seconds — with an audit trail that points to official filings, not web pages.

And the precision keeps improving. Our engineering team built an evolutionary optimization framework that continuously refines how the system formulates its queries, testing thousands of variations against real government data. Better questions yield more precise answers — and every iteration makes the next search sharper.

Section 04

Why This Matters

Enhanced Due Diligence

Gov Archive surfaces signals that aren't available through any other commercial source — OSHA violations, EPA compliance records, active litigation, licensing status across jurisdictions. All from authoritative government sources, all fully cited, all in seconds instead of hours.

As the system resolves more government records into the entity graph, these signals become part of the structured profile — risk indicators from government filings, delivered alongside the data compliance teams already use.

KYB Workflows

When automated checks can't find enough data to make a decision — which happens on a significant share of applications — Gov Archive provides the additional evidence that lets analysts resolve cases faster, with citations they can trust.

The goal isn't just faster reviews. It's fewer reviews — because more decisions can be made automatically when the data is there.

Case Study

Cannabis Dispensary Onboarding

A major payroll platform needed to onboard cannabis dispensaries, which meant verifying businesses across a patchwork of state-by-state licensing regimes. Many of these businesses couldn't be verified through standard data sources — the licensing records simply didn't exist in any commercial database.

We sourced cannabis licenses from every state, ingested them into the archive, and made them queryable in a matter of weeks. Without this infrastructure, that would have required custom scrapers for every state agency. With it, the next request like this gets faster — the system learns from every new source it ingests.

These are the use cases we know about today. The archive spans virtually every domain of regulated business activity in America — and we're actively discovering what else it makes possible.
